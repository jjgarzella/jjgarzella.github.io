This is my advice for undergraduate math majors at UCSD[1], based on conversations I've had. My biggest piece of advice boils down to one thing.

Don't buy into the GPA grindset mindset

The biggest mistake I see undergrads make at UCSD is focusing too much on classes and grades. It's a weird thing to say, right? You'd think that classes are the most important thing about school. But it's not; the most important thing about school is knowledge and skills. Grades are at best just a rough approximation.

The very best, most impactful experiences I had in my math major weren't things that were listed on the major requirements chart that the advisor gives you when you're a freshman. They were seminars, conversations, reading courses, graduate courses, hackathons, research projects, practically anything that wasn't an undergraduate class. Not that the core classes that I took were bad, they just weren't the whole thing, or even (by the end) the main thing. And probably the most lasting thing was the relationships I made with the people I met.

So that's my biggest piece of advice: don't spend so much time grinding on classes and grades that you shortchange yourself from all of these other things. Try out the Putnam, do competitions like the MCM or hackathons. Go to seminars, go to SUMS events and math department meet and greets. Try to get to know your TA's and professors and classmates. Take reading courses. Take graduate classes. Do an honors thesis. Do research if you get the opportunity. Obviously you don't have to do everything, but you're doing yourself a disservice if you don't try something.

I have three corollaries of this broader point, all relating to specific things that come up often.

Corollary 1: Take Reading Courses

A reading course is basically an informal independent study. Normally, the mentor picks a topic (or the student chooses from a list of topics given by the mentor). The mentor assigns reading material, and the student(s) meet to discuss what they've read with the mentor at least once a week. There are many variations on this concept, and it looks very different depending on who's doing it. The topics in these reading courses tend to be very interesting—many times such topics are foundational for modern research, yet won't appear in any traditional class.

So how does one do a reading course? Well, sometimes they are announced, like with the DRP at UCSD which happens most quarters. Beyond this, you can just ask any professor/TA that you have if they'll do a reading course with you. Often they can, or if they're too busy they'll refer you to someone they know who has more time.

I asked my professor/TA to do a reading course and they said no (and didn't offer any alternatives), what do I do now?

Sorry about this. At UCSD, there's a chunk of people in the math department who are afraid of being overwhelmed by undergrads and will say no to pretty much anything (if you didn't know, UCSD has about one order of magnitude more math majors than most comparable schools in the USA).

If you can understand the material in core math courses like Math 100 or Math 140, there's almost certainly someone in the department who will do a reading course with you. So I'd encourage you to ask a different professor/TA, or else wait and ask next quarter's professor/TA.

Corollary 2: Take Grad Classes

Graduate courses cover more advanced (and thus more interesting!) topics than standard undergrad courses. Also, the class sizes are smaller, so it's easier to get to know the professor and the other students. I especially recommend taking graduate classes to anyone who is deciding if they want to graduate school.

Many people assume that undergraduates are not allowed to take graduate courses. But this is actually dead wrong—generally speaking if an undergrad has the consent of the instructor, they can submit an EASy request and take the class. Practically speaking, what you should do if you want to go this route is:

  1. learn the necessary background (often varies from the listed prerequisites, ask a mentor if you're not sure)

  2. email the professor, letting them know that you have the background (the easiest thing is if you have another mentor who can vouch for you, like a professor of a previous class or a reading course mentor)

  3. when the professor responds positively, then submit the EASy request

Finally, given the difficulty, I recommend taking just one graduate class to start. After you've taken one, you'll understand the time commitment and be able to judge how many you can take in the future. For example, if you enjoyed Math 140 (resp. Math 100), try taking Math 240 (resp. Math 200).

Corollary 3: Leave some time for rest

This is a more standard piece of advice, so I won't belabor the point. In the quarter system especially, it's really easy to load up your schedule with classes (and all the other things) and not have time for anything else. Know that it's ok to not do every single class and activity. Also know that if you mess up one semester, it's not the end of the world.

One strategy that I like is to pick one day a week (probably Saturday or Sunday), and choose to do no math at all on that day. I've done this for a while and it's never been a hindrance to productivity.

In the grand scheme of life, learning to rest well and manage your time is more important than anything else that's on this webpage.
